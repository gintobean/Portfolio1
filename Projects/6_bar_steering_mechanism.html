Novel 6-Bar Steering Mechanism Analysis

Introduction

My design for a vehicular steering mechanism was a 6-bar rack-and-pinion mechanism that could achieve angles closer to the Ackermann Property than the more common 4-bar trapezoidal mechanism. Simple and symmetrical, this mechanism could be used in future vehicles to minimize the risk of skidding. With this project, my goal was to take an idea from scratch, then research and prototype and publish in a research-style article (link above). I strived to gain more experience in reading technical literature and applying knowledge from field professionals, which would be useful in R&D.

Initial Concept

As I was showering one night, I suddenly had a thought: how do cars turn both wheels without slipping or skidding? Doesn’t each wheel have a different turn radius? After some research, I learned of the Ackermann Steering Mechanism, a simple linkage that allows the inner and outer wheels to turn at different angles according to the Ackermann Property (more on that later), preventing skidding and improving safety.

Research

After conducting a mathematical analysis on the 6-bar mechanism, I found closed loop solutions yielding the turning angles of the wheels with respect to each other, then compared them to the “ideal” Ackermann profile. Using matlab, I developed an optimization program that inputted random linkage lengths and determined which combinations would yield the lowest error from “ideal.” It was determined that a 6-bar linkage with the dimensions below reduced the angular error by almost 90%, compared to the trapezoidal mechanism.

After calculating optimal dimensions for the 6-bar mechanism, I utilized solidworks to model the mechanism and print parts. The full print was in PLA. However, the grooves on the PLA presented the printed gears from smoothly meshing, so I purchased acrylic gears of the same dimensions. The result is pictured below: The final prototype consisted of acrylic gears and PLA linkages. An arm was added to the pinion to allow the attachment of a universal joint and steering wheel. This steering mechanism will be part of a larger electric car project.

Conclusion

In reality, most steering mechanisms will not function on acrylic and PLA due to high friction and thus heavy wear. However, this project provides insight on various, more complicated mechanisms that can be used in place of simpler, less accurate mechanisms, possibly eliminating the need for variable steering and thus reducing manufacturing costs.
